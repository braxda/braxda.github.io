Recruiting Maps

For this portion of the project, I developed interactive and static maps to visualize recruiting patterns for Michigan, Washington, Texas, and Alabama. The analysis explores trends in regional recruitment and how teams prioritize between local and out-of-state players. The maps provide insights into player distribution across the country, showing how competitive each team is in certain regions and their success in attracting recruits from highly talent-dense areas.

Recruiting Footprint Map

This map visualizes the geographic distribution of college football recruits for each school, using standard deviation ellipses to represent the spatial spread of recruited players. Each ellipse captures the core recruiting footprint for a program, showing the general area where most of their recruits are concentrated. Mean center points mark the average geographic location of recruits for each school, offering a quick visual cue to see where a program’s recruiting base is centered.

A few trends pop out from the map above, such as the relatively smaller diameter of Texas' standard deviation ellipse, both in height and width. A large portion of Texas' players are recruited from in-state, given that the Dallas-Fort Worth metro region is a hotbed of college football talent. Washington stands out, as the university itself is not even within it's own ellipse, representing a highly-decentralized recruiting footprint. A large portion of the players do hail from the west coast, but there are quite a few long-distance recruits from Texas, Hawaii, and even a German player. Michigan and Alabama unsurprisingly dominate the east-coast recruits from this group, given their proximity. Michigan focuses much more attention to the Northeast corridor than any other school, and Alabama holds most of their influence in the Southeast region of the country.

Recruiting Pathways Map

This hub-and-spoke map visualizes the connections between recruit's hometowns and the schools they signed with, illustrating the geographic reach and recruitment pipelines of each program. Each player is represented by a point at their hometown, with lines extending to their chosen program, revealing patterns of regional dominance, cross-country and international recruiting tendencies, and outlier signings. This map highlights the varying geographic strategies programs use to secure talent. Toggle the layers on the left panel to view other teams' data.

This map again highlights two broad patterns emerging from the data. Texas has focused much of their recruiting on in-state players and players from the southern half of the country. Alabama is similarly highly-regionalized, with many of the players coming from the Southeast and Central regions of the United States. Michigan and Washington, on the other-hand, appear to be taking a more country-wide approach to recruitment. These schools do not benefit from having proximity to talent-dense regions of the countries, so are forced to cast a wider net all across the country, and world.

Visit this map's project details on ArcGIS Online.

Recruiting Heatmap

This map visualizes player recruiting hotspots using a heatmap to identify regions with the highest concentration of college football recruits. Warmer colors indicate areas with a dense supply of talent, while cooler areas represent regions with fewer recruits.

The visualization highlights well-established recruiting powerhouses like Los Angeles, Dallas-Forth Worth, and the Southeast, namely Alabama and the Atlanta Metro Area. It also reveals smaller, less traditional hotspots in areas like the Northeast corridor, Seattle, and Detroit—patterns likely driven, in part, by the presence of nearby College Football Playoff contenders from this season.

Visit this map's project details on ArcGIS Online.

1-Hour Drive-Time Isochrones by Team

These static images were created in ArcGIS to visualize 1-hour drive-time isochrones around each program. Each dot marks the hometown of players signed by the school featured on the map. A single dot may represent multiple players from the same town. The map is school-specific and does not display recruits from other programs in the area. To explore the underlying data and interact with the maps directly, click any image to view the live version on ArcGIS Online.
